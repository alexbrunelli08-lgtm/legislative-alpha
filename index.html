How this works

01 — Legislative momentum score

A 0–100 score built from real, directly observable signals: the bill's current stage (introduced → committee → passed a chamber → signed), its real cosponsor count, and whether it saw action in the last 14 days. This is a progress signal, not a predictive model.

02 — Congressional trades, both chambers

Every trade disclosed in Periodic Transaction Reports (STOCK Act filings) over the data window is included: Senate reports are scraped from efdsearch.senate.gov, and House reports are parsed from the Clerk's e-filed disclosure PDFs. House filings submitted on paper are scanned images and cannot be parsed — the daily run logs how many were skipped. Trades in a sector's tracked companies are attributed to that sector; everything else appears under "Other / Unclassified."

03 — The lobbying–bill–trade nexus

Lobbying rows come from the public Lobbying Disclosure Act database, matched by each sector's tracked companies. When a filing's own activity description names a bill number we track, that filing is linked to the bill — open a bill to see exactly who reported lobbying on it. A bill is also flagged when a tracked trade in its sector was disclosed in the 45 days before introduction. These are disclosed-record joins, not causal claims.

About this data. Bills and amendments come from the Congress.gov API. Trades come from Senate financial disclosures. Lobbying comes from the Senate LDA API. Data refreshes once daily via a scheduled job. Sector definitions (keyword lists and tracked companies) are hand-curated starting points — review and expand them in scripts/sectors.json.
